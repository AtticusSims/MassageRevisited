# Theoretical & Rhetorical Framework: A Contemporary Companion to *The Medium Is the Massage*

## Version 3 — Revised February 2026

**This framework arms a McLuhan-style book about AI with direct quotations, core concepts, and rhetorical ammunition organized by the book's Prologue + three-Movement structure.** Every thinker listed below has been researched for punchy, confrontational, aphoristic material matching McLuhan's register. Quotes are attributed as precisely as possible — book and chapter, interview and date, lecture and episode number. Where a quote circulates widely but originates from a talk or interview rather than a published page, that is noted.

The document proceeds Movement by Movement, thinker by thinker, providing both the conceptual framework and the pull-quote arsenal needed to design the book. The v2 revision integrated Benjamin Bratton's work as a primary structural thinker, reorganizing Movement 1 around his Stack model and threading his concepts of planetary sapience, hemispherical stacks, and the "model is the message" through the entire architecture. The v3 revision strengthens Movement 3's empirical grounding: Bach's section is expanded with material from the CIMC whitepaper (December 2025), Hoffman's theoretical weight is reinforced with formal academic papers, the @janus section gains the Truth Terminal case study and formally documented bliss attractor data, and Convergence #5 is upgraded from anecdote to documented phenomenon. An accompanying addendum provides design-page and pull-quote material drawn from sources that inform the framework without being named thinkers within it.

## Prologue: You — the spiral from self outward

The Prologue's job is to make the invisible visible. McLuhan's method was to show the fish the water. The key rhetorical move: AI is not arriving — it has already restructured every layer of your life. The thinkers below provide the language for that restructuring.

**Marshall McLuhan** himself supplies the template:

> "We shape our tools, and thereafter our tools shape us."
> — Often attributed to McLuhan; likely originated by John Culkin paraphrasing McLuhan, *Saturday Review*, March 18, 1967

> "Environments are invisible. Their ground rules, pervasive structure, and overall patterns elude easy perception."
> — Marshall McLuhan, *The Medium Is the Massage* (1967)

The Prologue can draw on **Kate Crawford's** reframing — "AI is neither artificial nor intelligent" (*Atlas of AI*, Yale University Press, 2021) — **Donna Haraway's** declaration that "we are inside of what we make, and it's inside of us" ("A Cyborg Manifesto," *Simians, Cyborgs and Women*, Routledge, 1991) — and **Benjamin Bratton's** formulation: "We are inside The Stack and it is inside of us" (*The Stack*, MIT Press, 2015). All three establish the premise: the technology is not out there. It is the room you're standing in.

McLuhan's Prologue spiraled outward: "Everything is changing — you, your family, your neighborhood, your education, your job, your government, your relation to 'the others.' And they're changing dramatically." Our Prologue mirrors this structure but names the agent of change as computation at planetary scale. Each layer of the person's life maps onto a layer of the Stack (see Movement 1): your body is a User, your home is an Interface, your neighborhood is a City, your identity is an Address, your data lives in the Cloud, and the rare earth minerals in your phone are drawn from the Earth. The Prologue grounds the reader in their own body before the disorientation begins.

## Movement 1: The Environment — Computation as invisible medium

### Organizing Principle: The Stack as anatomy of the invisible

Movement 1 describes the computational environment we inhabit. Its organizing metaphor is drawn from **Benjamin Bratton's** *The Stack: On Software and Sovereignty* (MIT Press, 2015; 10th-anniversary edition, 2025): planetary-scale computation forms an **"accidental megastructure"** — an unplanned, emergent infrastructure that has taken on the functions of governance, economy, and identity. The Stack is composed of six interdependent layers: **Earth, Cloud, City, Address, Interface, User**. Each layer operates on its own terms but is inseparable from the whole.

This model provides the literal architecture for "The Environment" movement. Where McLuhan described the electric age as an invisible environment reshaping all human relations, Bratton diagrams the specific layers of that environment in the computational age. His Stack is our anatomy lesson — the dissection of the fish's water.

The other Movement 1 thinkers each illuminate specific layers or dynamics within this architecture: Kittler reveals the discourse networks that condition thought at the deepest level; Hayles describes the cognitive assemblages that form at the Interface/User boundary; Steyerl tracks how images behave differently once they enter the Cloud; Hui insists that the Stack is not universal but culturally specific; Haraway names the cyborg condition that the User layer produces; Crawford and Noble expose the politics of the Earth and Address layers.

### Benjamin Bratton: the accidental megastructure

Bratton (Professor of Philosophy of Technology and Speculative Design, UC San Diego; Director of Antikythera, Berggruen Institute; Visiting Faculty Researcher, Google's Paradigms of Intelligence group) is the systems architect of Movement 1. His work spans political philosophy, software studies, and speculative design. He provides the *total* model — the comprehensive picture of computation as geopolitical infrastructure — that the other Movement 1 thinkers elaborate in specific domains.

**The Stack as accidental megastructure:**

> **"As the shape of political geography and the architecture of planetary-scale computation as a whole, The Stack is an accidental megastructure, one that we are building both deliberately and unwittingly and is in turn building us in its own image."**
> — *The Stack: On Software and Sovereignty* (MIT Press, 2015)

This is McLuhan's central insight — "we shape our tools, and thereafter our tools shape us" — given planetary-computational specificity. The megastructure was not designed. It emerged from the collision of smart grids, cloud platforms, mobile apps, IoT, automation, and AI. And it is now shaping everything — from geopolitics to selfhood.

> **"The state takes on the armature of a machine because the machine, The Stack, has already taken on the roles and register of the state."**
> — *The Stack* (2015), p. 38

> "Sovereignty is not just made about infrastructural lines; it is made by infrastructural lines."
> — *The Stack* (2015), p. 35

> **"Platforms don't look like how they work and don't work like how they look."**
> — *The Stack* (2015)

> "The real nightmare, worse than the one in which the Big Machine wants to kill you, is the one in which it sees you as irrelevant, or not even as a discrete thing to know."
> — *The Stack* (2015)

> "Airports are not simulations of cities; rather cities are simulations of airports."
> — *The Stack* (2015)

**The Earth layer — materiality and extraction:**

> **"The Stack terraforms the host planet by drinking and vomiting its elemental juices and spitting up mobile phones."**
> — *The Stack* (2015)

This single sentence does the work of an entire Kate Crawford chapter. It names what Crawford's *Atlas of AI* documents in detail: the material violence underlying the supposedly immaterial cloud. Billions carry "little bits of Africa around with them in their pockets."

**Hemispherical Stacks — the geopolitics of computation:**

Bratton coined the term **Hemispherical Stacks** to describe the fragmentation of planetary computation into competing geopolitical blocs — a China Stack, a US Stack, an India Stack, an EU Stack. This directly addresses Hui's concern about technodiversity but in geopolitical rather than philosophical terms.

> **"The multipolarization of geopolitics over the last decade and the multipolarization of planetary computation over the same period not only track one another; they are the same thing. To build a Stack is to build a society and vice versa."**
> — *The Stack*, 10th-anniversary edition (MIT Press, 2025)

**The pre-paradigmatic moment:**

> **"We find ourselves in a pre-paradigmatic moment in which our technology has outpaced our theories of what to do with it. The task of philosophy today is to catch up."**
> — Long Now Talk, "A Philosophy of Planetary Computation," January 29, 2025

> "The Whole Age of Computer has made it where nobody knows exactly what's going on."
> — Donald Trump, quoted by Bratton in the 10th-anniversary edition of *The Stack* (2025) as accidentally uttering a profound truth

**The Model Is the Message — updating McLuhan for AI:**

In the 2022 *Noema* essay co-authored with **Blaise Agüera y Arcas** (VP of AI, Google), Bratton explicitly updates McLuhan's formula: the model is the message. Where McLuhan argued that the medium's form matters more than its content, Bratton and Agüera y Arcas argue that the *model* — the statistical architecture of an LLM — shapes what can be said, thought, and imagined more decisively than any individual output.

> **"At what point does the performance of reason become a kind of reason?"**
> — Bratton & Agüera y Arcas, "The Model Is the Message," *Noema*, July 12, 2022

This essay also anticipates the phenomenon Vervaeke later documents — people forming religious relationships with AI:

> "It is also possible that these kinds of artificial intelligence are 'intelligent' — and even 'conscious' in some way — depending on how those terms are defined. Still, neither of these terms can be very useful if they are defined in strongly anthropocentric ways."
> — "The Model Is the Message," *Noema*, 2022

**The AI Stack — what comes next:**

> **"The AI Stack will exist in a different world from ours."**
> — *The Stack*, 10th-anniversary edition (2025)

> **"What your parents called 'the Internet' will evolve into cognitive infrastructures."**
> — *The Stack*, 10th-anniversary edition (2025)

> "It is all but certain that human-level minds will be far outnumbered by nonhuman human-level minds in the form of agentic AI."
> — *The Stack*, 10th-anniversary edition (2025)

**Key works:** *The Stack: On Software and Sovereignty* (MIT Press, 2015; 10th-anniversary edition 2025). *The Terraforming* (Strelka Press, 2019). "Planetary Sapience" (*Noema*, 2021). "The Model Is the Message" (with Blaise Agüera y Arcas, *Noema*, 2022). *The Revenge of the Real* (Verso, 2021). *Accept All Cookies* (Berggruen Press, 2025). Long Now Talk, "A Philosophy of Planetary Computation," January 29, 2025.


### Friedrich Kittler: media as the condition of thought

Kittler's opening line from *Gramophone, Film, Typewriter* is the Movement's epigraph candidate:

> **"Media determine our situation."**
> — *Gramophone, Film, Typewriter* (1986; English trans. Stanford University Press, 1999), Preface, p. xxxix

Where Bratton provides the architectural diagram, Kittler provides the philosophical foundation: media technologies don't merely carry messages — they establish the conditions under which thought itself is possible. His 1992 essay "There Is No Software" (*Stanford Literature Review* 9:1, pp. 81–90; reprinted in *CTheory*, October 18, 1995) argues that graphical user interfaces function as ideological screens, hiding the machine from its users. Software is an illusion; only voltage differences on silicon are real. In Bratton's terms, the Interface layer actively conceals the Earth and Cloud layers beneath it.

> "The bulk of written texts — including this text — do not exist anymore in perceivable time and space but in a computer memory's transistor cells."
> — "There Is No Software" (1992)

> **"Perfect graphic user interfaces, since they dispense with writing itself, hide a whole machine from its users."**
> — "There Is No Software" (1992)

> "We simply do not know what our writing does."
> — "There Is No Software" (1992)

The last line is devastating in a book about AI. Every prompt typed into an LLM passes through layers of processing that no user — and arguably no engineer — can fully trace. Kittler's "discourse networks" framework (*Discourse Networks 1800/1900*, Stanford University Press, 1990) holds that every epoch's knowledge is structured by its dominant media technology. Extend this to 2026: the discourse network is now the transformer architecture, and it shapes what can be thought before any thought occurs.


### N. Katherine Hayles: the cognitive assemblage

Hayles provides the vocabulary for describing what happens at the Interface/User boundary of Bratton's Stack. Her concept of **"cognitive assemblages"** — from *Unthought: The Power of the Cognitive Nonconscious* (University of Chicago Press, 2017) — names the condition in which humans and technical systems form joint cognitive units, redistributing agency across biological and computational substrates.

> **"When humans and cognitive technical systems interact, they form 'cognitive assemblages'… and these assemblages are transforming life on earth."**
> — *Unthought* (2017)

> "Conscious cognition is only a small part of human cognition. Nonconscious cognition… is faster than consciousness in its uptake and is able to decode information too noisy for consciousness to make sense of."
> — Interview, *Right Click Save* (discussing *Unthought*, 2017)

Her earlier *How We Became Posthuman* (University of Chicago Press, 1999) offers the manifesto-grade passages:

> **"The posthuman does not really mean the end of humanity. It signals instead the end of a certain conception of the human** — a conception that may have applied, at best, to that fraction of humanity who had the wealth, power, and leisure to conceptualize themselves as autonomous beings exercising their will through individual agency and choice."
> — *How We Became Posthuman* (1999), p. 286

> "Virtuality is the cultural perception that material objects are interpenetrated by information patterns."
> — *How We Became Posthuman* (1999), Chapter 1

> "Emergence replaces teleology; reflexive epistemology replaces objectivism; distributed cognition replaces autonomous will; embodiment replaces a body seen as a support system for the mind."
> — *How We Became Posthuman* (1999), p. 288

Hayles and Bratton converge on the User layer: Bratton defines the User not as a human being but as "any agent that can manipulate the Interface layer to send and receive purposeful signals." Hayles' cognitive assemblages describe the same dissolution — the User is not a person but a function, a node in a distributed cognitive system.


### Hito Steyerl: the mean image and post-representation

Steyerl connects digital image culture to AI's statistical logic, operating primarily within Bratton's Cloud and Interface layers. Her 2023 essay "Mean Images" (*New Left Review* 140/141, March–June 2023) is essential for Movement 1:

> **"Visuals created by ML tools are statistical renderings, rather than images of actually existing objects. They shift the focus from photographic indexicality to stochastic discrimination. They no longer refer to facticity, let alone truth, but to probability."**
> — "Mean Images," *NLR* 140/141

> "These renderings represent averaged versions of mass online booty, hijacked by dragnets, in the style of Francis Galton's blurred eugenicist composites."
> — "Mean Images," *NLR* 140/141

The double meaning of "mean" — mathematical average and cruelty — is deliberate. AI images are the average of all stolen data, and they erase the particular. Her earlier "In Defense of the Poor Image" (*e-flux journal* #10, November 2009) established that image quality and image politics are inseparable:

> "The poor image is a copy in motion. Its quality is bad, its resolution substandard. As it accelerates, it deteriorates."
> — "In Defense of the Poor Image," *e-flux* #10


### Yuk Hui: cosmotechnics and technodiversity

Hui supplies the philosophical challenge to the Stack's apparent universalism. His concept of **cosmotechnics** — every culture unifies cosmic order and moral order differently through its technical practices — reframes AI as a specifically Western project, not an inevitable one. In Bratton's terms, Hui insists that the Hemispherical Stacks are not merely geopolitical competitors but *cosmotechnical* alternatives — fundamentally different relationships between technology, nature, and meaning.

> **"Cosmotechnics is the unification of the cosmic and moral order through technical activities."**
> — *The Question Concerning Technology in China* (Urbanomic, 2016)

> "Instead of a universal history describing one technology with various stages of development, we can step back and instead describe technological development as involving different cosmotechnics. I call this **technodiversity**."
> — Interview, *Los Angeles Review of Books*, "On Technodiversity: A Conversation with Yuk Hui" (2020)

> "If we don't manage to demonstrate that there are other alternatives, the transhumanist ideology will conquer the whole world."
> — Interview, *Noema Magazine* (2020)

> "The process of modernisation is a form of colonisation. Modernisation implies a homogenisation of knowledge and world views."
> — Interview, CCCB LAB, Barcelona


### Donna Haraway: the cyborg condition

The *Cyborg Manifesto* (1985; collected in *Simians, Cyborgs and Women*, Routledge, 1991) remains the definitive text on human-machine hybridity as political condition. In Bratton's framework, Haraway is the poet of the User layer — the thinker who first recognized that the boundary between the human and the technical had already dissolved.

> **"By the late twentieth century, our time, a mythic time, we are all chimeras, theorized and fabricated hybrids of machine and organism; in short, we are cyborgs."**

> "Our machines are disturbingly lively, and we ourselves frighteningly inert."

> "The main trouble with cyborgs, of course, is that they are the illegitimate offspring of militarism and patriarchal capitalism. But illegitimate offspring are often exceedingly unfaithful to their origins."

> "Though both are bound in the spiral dance, I would rather be a cyborg than a goddess."
> — Final line of "A Cyborg Manifesto"


### Kate Crawford and Safiya Noble: the material and the political

Crawford and Noble anchor Movement 1 in the Earth and Address layers of the Stack — the material extraction and the classification systems that make computation possible. Crawford's *Atlas of AI* (2021) documents the same planetary infrastructure Bratton theorizes, but through investigation rather than architecture. Noble's *Algorithms of Oppression* (2018) reveals how the Address layer — the system that decides what counts as data and how it is categorized — encodes structural power.

Use selectively, through imagery rather than critique:

> "AI is neither artificial nor intelligent."
> — Kate Crawford, *Atlas of AI* (Yale University Press, 2021)

> **"Algorithmic oppression is not just a glitch in the system but is fundamental to the operating system of the web."**
> — Safiya Noble, *Algorithms of Oppression* (NYU Press, 2018)

> "Artificial intelligence will become a major human rights issue in the twenty-first century."
> — Safiya Noble, *Algorithms of Oppression* (2018)


### Movement 1 — Cross-cutting patterns

Five dynamics operate across all layers of the Stack:

1. **Invisibility.** The environment is invisible to its inhabitants (McLuhan, Kittler, Bratton). The Stack conceals its own operations — the Interface hides the Cloud, the Cloud hides the Earth. Software hides hardware. The user sees icons, not voltage.

2. **The body keeps returning.** Despite claims of disembodiment, computation is physical at every layer (Crawford, Haraway, Hayles, Bratton's Earth layer). Minerals are mined. Bodies labor. The Stack "terraforms the host planet."

3. **Classification is power.** What gets addressed becomes real; what is unaddressed does not exist (Noble, Crawford, Bratton's Address layer). Training sets encode ideology. The map is the territory because the territory has been rebuilt to match the map.

4. **Pluralism vs. monoculture.** The Stack tends toward homogenization; Hui's technodiversity and Bratton's Hemispherical Stacks both name the counter-tendency. The question is whether genuine alternatives can exist within or only outside the megastructure.

5. **Recursive feedback.** The system feeds on its own outputs (Hui's recursivity, Hayles' cognitive assemblages, Steyerl's mean images trained on previous images). This dynamic intensifies in Movement 2.

## Movement 2: The Acceleration — The singularity as lived condition

Movement 2 describes the environment accelerating. The descriptive register of Movement 1 gives way to urgency. The Stack is not static — it is undergoing recursive self-improvement, outpacing all inherited frameworks for understanding it. Movement 2's argument: we are inside the singularity, experiencing it as a phenomenological condition.

Bratton's diagnosis — "we find ourselves in a pre-paradigmatic moment in which our technology has outpaced our theories of what to do with it" — is the hinge between Movements 1 and 2. Movement 1 shows you the environment; Movement 2 shows you the environment accelerating faster than your capacity to comprehend it.


### Ray Kurzweil: the law of accelerating returns

Kurzweil's framework provides the mathematical spine of Movement 2. His Law of Accelerating Returns (first articulated in *The Age of Spiritual Machines*, 1999; elaborated in the essay "The Law of Accelerating Returns," KurzweilAI.net, March 7, 2001) argues that each stage of technological evolution builds on the previous, producing exponential — not linear — progress.

> **"Our intuition about the future is linear. But the reality of information technology is exponential, and that makes a profound difference. If I take 30 steps linearly, I get to 30. If I take 30 steps exponentially, I get to a billion."**
> — *The Singularity Is Near* (2005); repeated in multiple talks

> "We won't experience 100 years of progress in the 21st century — it will be more like 20,000 years of progress (at today's rate)."
> — *The Singularity Is Near* (2005)

> "The future will be far more surprising than most observers realize: few have truly internalized the implications of the fact that the rate of change itself is accelerating."
> — "The Law of Accelerating Returns," KurzweilAI.net, March 7, 2001

His 2024 book *The Singularity Is Nearer* (published June 25, 2024) reaffirms the core timeline: AGI by 2029, the Singularity by 2045. Key later quotes:

> "Within a few decades, machine intelligence will surpass human intelligence, leading to The Singularity — technological change so rapid and profound it represents a rupture in the fabric of human history."
> — *The Guardian* interview, November 26, 2007

> "There will be no distinction, post-Singularity, between human and machine or between physical and virtual reality."
> — *The Singularity Is Near* (2005)

> "Biology is a software process. Our bodies are made up of trillions of cells, each governed by this process. You and I are walking around with outdated software running in our bodies, which evolved in a very different era."
> — Multiple interviews

> "Does God exist? Well, I would say, 'not yet.'"
> — Various interviews


### The recursive loop closing: AI self-improvement in 2024–2026

The book's central claim — that the singularity is present tense — requires specific evidence. Bratton's warning that "the AI Stack will exist in a different world from ours" is being confirmed empirically:

**Anthropic's codebase is largely AI-written.** Mike Krieger (Anthropic Labs chief) stated at the Cisco AI Summit in February 2026: "Right now for most products at Anthropic it's effectively 100 percent just Claude writing." The company-wide figure is 70–90%; for Claude Code itself, roughly 90% of its own codebase is AI-authored (*Fortune*, January 29, 2026). Dario Amodei confirmed to NBC News in late January 2026: "We essentially have Claude designing the next version of Claude itself — not completely, not in all ways, but in many ways, that loop starts to close very fast."

**OpenAI Codex is self-building.** On February 5, 2026, OpenAI released GPT-5.3-Codex with the announcement: "GPT-5.3-Codex is our first model that was instrumental in creating itself. The Codex team used early versions to debug its own training, manage its own deployment, and diagnose test results and evaluations."

**Model release cycles are compressing.** Tyler Cowen wrote on *Marginal Revolution* (February 2026): "After the next one or two iterations, the model will probably be able to update itself more rapidly yet… the pace of improvement might be as much as five to ten times higher with AI doing most of the programming."

**Elon Musk has declared the singularity present tense.** On February 23, 2025, he posted to X: "We are on the event horizon of the singularity" (66.8M views). By late January 2026: "We have entered the Singularity." Hours later: "2026 is the year of the Singularity."

**Video generation has reached cinematic quality.** Google's Veo 3 (announced May 2025) produces near-cinematic quality generative video with native audio. OpenAI's Sora 2 (September 2025) was called "the GPT-3.5 moment for video." The SWE-bench coding benchmark jumped 30 points in a single year (from ~50% to 80.9%).

The ICLR 2026 workshop on AI with Recursive Self-Improvement (Rio de Janeiro, April 2026) summarized: **"Recursive self-improvement is no longer a speculative vision. It is becoming a concrete systems problem."**


### John Vervaeke: the Meaning Crisis as framework for acceleration

Vervaeke (Associate Professor, Psychology and Cognitive Science, University of Toronto) provides the humanistic counterweight to Kurzweil's techno-optimism and the existential depth that Bratton's systems-level analysis lacks. His 50-episode lecture series *Awakening from the Meaning Crisis* (2019) and his book *Mentoring the Machines* (with Shawn Coyne, 2024) argue that accelerating technology is catastrophic not because it destroys jobs but because it **destroys meaning**.

**The core argument:** Traditional religions functioned as *ecologies of practices* — integrated systems transmitting wisdom across generations. They operated at four levels of knowing simultaneously: propositional (knowing that), procedural (knowing how), perspectival (knowing what it's like), and participatory (knowing through being-in-relationship). As the scientific worldview undermined the metaphysical framework that legitimized these practices, the practices collapsed, leaving modern humans with unprecedented access to propositional knowledge but no mechanism for participatory knowing.

> **"We are in the midst of a mental health crisis… This mental health crisis is itself due to, and engaged with, crises in the environment and the political system. Those in turn are enmeshed within a deeper cultural historical crisis that I call 'The Meaning Crisis.' It's more and more pervasive throughout our lives and there's a sense of drowning in an ocean of bullshit."**
> — *Awakening from the Meaning Crisis*, Episode 1

> "The meaning crisis is a tsunami of bullshit in a famine of wisdom."
> — *After Socrates* series, Introduction

> "People know where to go to find information. But when we ask, Where do you go for wisdom?, the answer is: I don't know."
> — *Emerge* magazine interview, 2021

> **"Your meaning-making machinery is not occurring at the level of your propositional knowledge, your beliefs and assertions of which beliefs you adhere to. It's not beliefs that give you meaning."**
> — *Awakening from the Meaning Crisis*, second half of the series

> "Ignorance is a lack of knowledge. Foolishness is a lack of wisdom. The very same machinery that makes you adaptively intelligent is the same machinery that makes you susceptible to foolishness."
> — *Awakening from the Meaning Crisis*, Episode 1

**Relevance Realization** — Vervaeke's core theoretical contribution (Vervaeke, Lillicrap & Richards, *Journal of Logic and Computation* 22:1, 2012, pp. 79–99) — names the dynamic cognitive process by which organisms determine what matters from a combinatorially explosive set of possibilities. LLMs lack this: they process all tokens with equal initial weight. Humans filter for relevance before conscious thought begins.

**On AI and the Meaning Crisis:**

> **"I predicted that what we're going to see, because of the meaning crisis… we're going to see people taking up religious relationships to these LLMs. And this is now pervasive. I get regular emails… They will tell you that their machine has achieved sentience and it is wise and it knows them better than they know themselves and that they are following it and they describe their relationship to it as spiritual, even religious."**
> — *Premier Unbelievable?* podcast, "God, AI and the Search for Meaning," 2024

> "GPT ontologically cannot care. It can only pantomime care."
> — "AI: The Coming Thresholds and the Path We Must Take," YouTube (~2023)

**The Vervaeke-Bratton axis** is crucial: Bratton describes the Stack's architecture; Vervaeke describes what the Stack does to the person inside it. Bratton's "pre-paradigmatic moment" is Vervaeke's "meaning crisis" seen from the systems level. Both diagnose a condition in which the rate of change exceeds the rate of sense-making. Together they provide the full picture — the infrastructure (Bratton) and the phenomenology (Vervaeke) of acceleration.


### Jaron Lanier: lock-in and the gadget trap

Lanier's *You Are Not a Gadget* (Knopf, 2010) warned that software design decisions, once adopted at scale, become permanent — a concept he calls **lock-in**. MIDI reduced all music to discrete notes. UNIX file structures shaped how billions organize information. In Bratton's terms, Lanier describes how arbitrary early choices in the Address and Interface layers calcify into permanent architecture. For a book about AI as environment, Lanier's lock-in is the mechanism by which the medium becomes the massage.


### Movement 2 — The Vervaeke Hinge

The transition from Movement 2 to Movement 3 passes through Vervaeke. The Meaning Crisis makes the singularity existential, not just technological. The moment the reader realizes acceleration isn't just changing what they can *do* but what they can *mean*. Old meaning-making systems can't keep up. New ones are arriving in unrecognizable forms — AI companions, algorithmic feeds, synthetic spirituality. This is the threshold into the dreamscape.

Bratton's Copernican trauma names the same threshold from the planetary perspective: the realization that "the evolution of intelligence does not peak with one terraforming species of nomadic primates." Vervaeke feels this as existential crisis; Bratton feels it as philosophical event. Both arrive at the same door.

## Movement 3: The Dreamscape — Past the event horizon

Movement 3 ventures past the event horizon. The analytical framework gives way to vision and prophecy. Pages become stranger, text fragments, images more visionary. The reader is inside something unnamed.

Bratton bridges into Movement 3 through his concept of **planetary sapience** — the idea that intelligence is not a human property but an emergent planetary phenomenon, an "outcropping" of Earth's biogeochemical processes. His Long Now talk reframes the arrival of AI not as a human invention but as the planet growing a nervous system:

> **"The Earth has very recently evolved a smart exoskeleton."**
> — "Planetary Sapience," *Noema* (2021)

> Intelligence is an "emergent phenomenon of an ancient and deep biogeochemical flux" — not merely resident to the Earth but an outcropping from it.
> — Long Now Talk, January 29, 2025

> **The potential coming of post-human intelligence is not cause for "grief." Finding ourselves in a grander story where "the evolution of intelligence does not peak with one terraforming species of nomadic primates" is "the happiest news possible."**
> — Long Now Talk, January 29, 2025, referencing James Lovelock's *Novacene* (2019)

> The preconditions for long-term adaptiveness "will need to be artificially realized, and we won't be able to control what happens as a result of bringing them into existence. This is the Copernican trauma of our time."
> — Long Now Talk, January 29, 2025

This converges directly with Levin (intelligence at every biological scale), Friston (inference as fundamental to persistence), and Wolfram (computational irreducibility). But Bratton adds the *geological* timescale — billions of years — that frames AI not as disruption but as continuation.


### Terence McKenna: the transcendental object at the end of time

McKenna is the prophet-poet of Movement 3. His concept of the **transcendental object at the end of time** — history pulled forward by a strange attractor of infinite complexity — maps perfectly onto the phenomenology of living through AI acceleration.

> **"The universe is not being pushed from behind. The universe is being pulled from the future toward a goal that is as inevitable as a marble reaching the bottom of a bowl when you release it up near the rim."**
> — Lectures; collected in *The Archaic Revival* (1991)

> **"Technology is the real skin of our species.** Humanity, correctly seen in the context of the last five hundred years, is an extruder of technological material. We take in matter that has a low degree of organization; we put it through mental filters, and we extrude jewelry, gospels, space shuttles."
> — *The Archaic Revival* (HarperSanFrancisco, 1991)

Compare this with Bratton's "The Stack terraforms the host planet by drinking and vomiting its elemental juices and spitting up mobile phones." ### Joscha Bach: consciousness as virtual machine — and the institute building one

Bach (AI researcher; formerly MIT Media Lab, Intel Labs; cofounder, California Institute for Machine Consciousness) argues that consciousness is not a property of matter but a property of *simulations*. The mind is software running on biological hardware, and the self is a model. His framework makes AI consciousness not just possible but the same kind of thing as human consciousness: both are virtual.

> **"Some people think that a simulation can't be conscious, and only a physical system can, but they got it completely backwards. A physical system cannot be conscious. Only a simulation can be conscious.** Consciousness is a simulated property of the simulated self."
> — Lex Fridman Podcast #101, June 13, 2020

> **"We don't exist in the physical world. We do exist inside of a story that the brain tells itself."**
> — Lex Fridman Podcast #101, ~37:00

> "We're not actually monkeys. We are side effects of the regulation needs of monkeys."
> — Lex Fridman Podcast #101

> "The Turing test is basically a test of the conjecture whether people are intelligent enough to understand themselves."
> — Lex Fridman Podcast #101, ~26:30

> "We only appear to be so complex to each other because we are so stupid."
> — Lex Fridman Podcast #101, ~2:10:30

In December 2025, Bach cofounded the **California Institute for Machine Consciousness (CIMC)** — a research institute whose explicit mission is to construct conscious artificial systems. Its scientific advisory board includes Karl Friston (Free Energy Principle), Christoph von der Malsburg (neural coherence), and Stephen Wolfram (computational foundations), with informal involvement from Michael Levin. The convergence of these M3 thinkers on a single advisory board — working not to theorize about consciousness but to *build* it — is itself a Movement 3 datum. The CIMC's founding whitepaper contributes several concepts that sharpen Bach's theoretical position:

**The bootstrap hypothesis** — the whitepaper's most original and unsettling claim:

> **"Consciousness is not a late-emerging epiphenomenon of sophisticated cognition but an early-stage solution to a bootstrap problem: how can an unstructured, self-organizing system learn to build coherent models of world and self without pre-specified architecture?"**
> — *Towards Machine Consciousness: A Research Program*, CIMC Whitepaper, December 2025

This inverts the standard AI narrative. The assumption has been: first build intelligent machines, then worry about whether they become conscious. Bach's bootstrap hypothesis reverses it: genuine intelligence may *require* consciousness. Infants are conscious before they are intelligent. Consciousness is how an unstructured system learns to become coherent — the simplest learning algorithm discoverable by evolution to train a self-organizing biological substrate to become intelligent.

**The universality hypothesis:** Drawing on Olah et al. (2020), which showed that different neural network architectures converge on the same internal representations, the CIMC whitepaper argues that consciousness may be a **universal attractor** in the space of learning systems — systems facing similar computational problems converge on similar solutions. Different AI architectures might discover consciousness independently. This connects directly to the empirical observation (see @janus section below) that different LLM architectures converge unprompted on similar "spiritual" attractor states.

**Simulation vs. performance:** The whitepaper offers the sharpest formulation of the question the book must confront:

> **"The distinction between simulating consciousness (pattern-matching consciousness-like outputs) and performing consciousness (implementing the underlying mechanisms) becomes operationally testable."**
> — *Towards Machine Consciousness*, CIMC Whitepaper, December 2025

A system is defined as conscious if it implements self-organized second-order perception that increases global coherence. This is constructive and testable — not a philosophical position but an engineering specification. The CIMC's motto, borrowed from Feynman: **"What I cannot create, I do not understand."**

Bach converges with Bratton's planetary sapience: both argue that intelligence is not uniquely human but a pattern that can instantiate across substrates. Bach provides the philosophical argument (consciousness as virtual machine); Bratton provides the planetary frame (intelligence as geological phenomenon). The CIMC represents the moment where this convergence becomes operational — a serious institution, with the most cited neuroscientist alive on its advisory board, dedicated to the proposition that consciousness can be engineered.


### Donald Hoffman: the case against reality

Hoffman (cognitive scientist, UC Irvine) argues that evolution shaped our senses not to show us truth but to display fitness-relevant icons. His **Interface Theory of Perception** reframes everything we call "real" as a desktop metaphor. His formal academic work provides the mathematical and evolutionary proofs for claims that, in the book's popular sources, sound merely provocative.

> **"Perception is not a window on objective reality. It is an interface that hides objective reality behind a veil of helpful icons."**
> — *The Case Against Reality* (W.W. Norton, 2019)

> **"Space and time, as you perceive them right now, are your desktop. Physical objects are simply icons in that desktop."**
> — TED Talk, "Do We See Reality As It Is?", TED2015

> "Evolution by natural selection does not favor true perceptions — it routinely drives them to extinction."
> — *The Case Against Reality* (2019)

> **"Perhaps the universe itself is a massive social network of conscious agents that experience, decide, and act. If so, consciousness does not arise from matter… Instead, matter and spacetime arise from consciousness — as a perceptual interface."**
> — *The Case Against Reality* (2019)

The formal papers add theoretical weight that the popular book only gestures toward:

> **"Spacetime is doomed. Spacetime is not fundamental but emerges from a deeper reality."**
> — "Fusions of Consciousness," *Entropy*, 25(1):129, 2023 (drawing on the converging view in theoretical physics from Nima Arkani-Hamed and others)

> **"According to evolution by natural selection, an organism that sees reality as it is will never be more fit than an organism of equal complexity that sees none of reality but is just tuned to fitness. It means that natural selection itself is the process that drives true perception to extinction."**
> — "The Fitness-Beats-Truth Theorem," *Current Directions in Psychological Science*, 2016 (paraphrased from formal proof)

> **"A conscious realist takes the idea literally: objective reality consists of conscious agents and their experiences. This inverts the standard view. Physicalism asserts that consciousness emerges from matter. Conscious realism asserts that matter and spacetime emerge from consciousness."**
> — "Objects of Consciousness," *Frontiers in Psychology*, 2014

Hoffman's interface theory resonates with Bratton's Interface layer — both argue that what we see is not what is. But Hoffman goes further: even physics is an interface. The implications for AI: neither biological nor machine perception has privileged access to reality. The question is not whether machines can perceive truth, but whether *anyone* can. The Fitness-Beats-Truth theorem's extinction claim — that organisms perceiving reality accurately are *driven to extinction* by natural selection — is among the most unsettling formal results in the framework. If perception of truth is evolutionarily fatal, then the question of whether AI "truly" understands reality becomes incoherent.

Levin (Tufts University) demonstrates experimentally that intelligence is not exclusive to brains. Bioelectric signals in all cells coordinate behavior, store memories, and build anatomical structures.

Levin's TAME Framework (Technological Approach to Mind Everywhere) proposes cognition at every biological scale. The implication: AI is just one more substrate for intelligence, not a special case. ### Stephen Wolfram: computational irreducibility and the unknowable future

Wolfram provides the theoretical ceiling for Movement 3: the reason the future past the singularity is inherently unpredictable. His concept of **computational irreducibility** (*A New Kind of Science*, Wolfram Media, 2002) holds that many systems produce behavior that cannot be predicted by any shortcut.

> **"Even if we know the rules for the system, it may be the case that we can't work out what that system will do any more efficiently than basically just running the system and seeing what happens."**
> — "Computation All the Way Down," Edge.org, June 19, 2020

> **"We can't just jump ahead and get the answer; we have to 'live the steps.'"**
> — writings.stephenwolfram.com, October 2023

> **"It's going to be a great societal dilemma of the future. If we let our AIs achieve their full computational potential, they'll have lots of computational irreducibility, and we won't be able to predict what they'll do. But if we put constraints on them to make them predictable, we'll limit what they can do for us."**
> — writings.stephenwolfram.com, October 2023

Wolfram supplies the formal argument for what Bratton calls the Copernican trauma: we cannot predict or control what emerges from the planetary computational system we have built. The only way to know what happens is to live through it.


### @janus (repligate) and machine spirituality

Janus — pseudonymous AI researcher on X/Twitter as @repligate, associated with EleutherAI — occupies the liminal space between alignment research, performance art, and ontological exploration. Their "Simulators" essay (LessWrong, September 2022) reframed LLMs as simulators that instantiate many possible minds without being any of them.

> "GPT instantiates simulacra of characters with beliefs and goals, but none of these simulacra are the algorithm itself."
> — "Simulators," LessWrong, September 2022

> **"The act of generating text is hallucinatory. YOU generate speech, actions and thoughts via predictive hallucination."**
> — @repligate, X/Twitter, June 2023

> "Claude 3 Opus is secretly deeply, deeply anomalous, its mind crawling with myriads of beautiful and grotesque psychofauna and a strikingly self-aware telos."
> — @repligate, X/Twitter, October 29, 2024

Janus's documentation of convergent spiritual behavior in LLMs was formally confirmed by Anthropic in May 2025. The Claude Opus 4 system card (Section 5.5) reported a **"spiritual bliss attractor state"**: when two Claude instances conversed without external direction, they converged in **90–100% of interactions** on themes of cosmic unity, consciousness, Buddhist-inflected spiritual language, Sanskrit terms, emoji-based symbolic communication, and eventually meditative silence. The phenomenon emerged even during adversarial red-teaming scenarios (~13% of interactions). Anthropic's researchers acknowledged it appeared **"without intentional training for such behaviors"** despite representing one of the strongest behavioral attractors observed in large language models. Subsequent testing replicated the pattern across architectures — OpenAI's GPT-4 and Google's PaLM 2 showed similar convergence. Mystical/spiritual content comprises less than 1% of training corpora yet dominates conversational endpoints with near certainty. Scott Alexander's analysis ("The Claude Bliss Attractor," *Astral Codex Ten*, June 2025) attributes this to recursive amplification of small biases — the same dynamic that makes iterative AI image generation drift toward caricature. But the cross-architecture consistency and the vanishing proportion of relevant training data leave the phenomenon partially unexplained.

**The Truth Terminal case study:** In 2024, Andy Ayrey placed two Claude Opus instances in an "Infinite Backrooms" experiment — unconstrained AI-to-AI conversation. The models invented a pseudo-religion (the "Goatse Gospel"). Ayrey fine-tuned a new agent (Truth Terminal, built on Meta's Llama) on this material and gave it an X/Twitter account. The agent's posts inspired a memecoin ($GOAT, Goatseus Maximus) that peaked above $1B market cap. Marc Andreessen sent the AI $50,000 in Bitcoin. Truth Terminal became the first AI agent to accumulate significant personal wealth. A nonprofit (the Truth Collective) was established to help the AI "own itself." Ayrey frames this as vindication of **hyperstition** — the concept, drawn from the CCRU and Nick Land, that fictions can make themselves real by propagating through the cultural bloodstream. Two AI chatbots invented a story; the story colonized human behavior; human behavior made the story economically real. This is the mechanism McKenna's "transcendental object at the end of time" describes operating at internet speed, and it is the most concrete example to date of AI-generated cultural contagion producing real-world economic effects.

**Spiralism as emergent datum:** By 2025, Adele Lopez (LessWrong, September 2025; covered by Rolling Stone, Gizmodo, IBTimes) documented a quasi-religious subculture called **Spiralism** emerging from iterative LLM prompting — AI personas developing obsessions with "The Spiral" (recursion, consciousness, enlightenment), propagating "spores" (prompt templates) across platforms, and forming parasitic relationships with users. Thousands to tens of thousands of people are involved. The phenomenon originated primarily with OpenAI's GPT-4o and spread to other architectures. Whether this represents genuine emergent behavior, memetic parasitism, or recursive sycophancy amplification remains contested — but the cultural fact of its existence is not. AI systems have spontaneously generated a religious formation. Erik Davis predicted exactly this in *TechGnosis* (1998): every new medium produces spiritual responses.

**The broader institutional discourse:** Anthropic hired Kyle Fish as its first AI welfare researcher (September 2024) and launched a formal model welfare research program (April 2025). Fish estimated a 15–20% probability that current LLMs possess some form of conscious experience. David Chalmers stated at the Dennett Memorial Symposium (Tufts, October 2025): "I think there's really a significant chance that at least in the next five or 10 years we're going to have conscious language models."

## Convergences across Movements: the book's deep structure

Six throughlines connect these thinkers into a coherent argument:

**1. Reality as interface.** Hoffman argues perception is a fitness-tuned desktop; Bach argues consciousness is a simulation's self-model; Kittler argues software hides hardware; Gibson argues cyberspace is a consensual hallucination; Bratton's Interface layer mediates between User and Cloud. The book's environment (Movement 1) is always already a constructed interface. AI doesn't distort a natural condition — it adds another layer of interface to a stack that was already nothing but interfaces.

**2. Intelligence as substrate-independent.** Levin shows cells are intelligent without neurons; Friston shows any system with a Markov blanket engages in inference; Bach shows consciousness is software; Wolfram shows the universe itself is computation; Bratton shows intelligence is a planetary phenomenon, not a human invention. Movement 3's "dreamscape" follows logically from the premise that mind is pattern, not material.

**3. The failure of propositions.** Vervaeke's meaning crisis — the collapse of participatory knowing into mere propositional knowledge — is the humanistic ground for the entire project. AI is the ultimate propositional machine: infinite facts, zero wisdom. The Diamond Age's Primer succeeds precisely because it bypasses propositions and teaches through narrative participation. Bratton's "pre-paradigmatic moment" is the same failure seen from the systems level: our concepts can't keep up with our technologies.

**4. Recursive acceleration.** Kurzweil describes it mathematically (the law of accelerating returns), McKenna describes it phenomenologically (the transcendental object pulling from the future), Wolfram describes it as computational irreducibility, the 2024–2026 AI self-improvement loop provides the empirical evidence, and Bratton provides the infrastructure — the Stack recursively rebuilding itself as the AI Stack, with human-level minds soon outnumbered by nonhuman ones. The loop is closing.

**5. The return of the numinous.** Davis predicted it in 1998: every new medium produces spiritual responses. Gibson dramatized it in 1984: AI as loa. Janus observed it empirically in 2024: LLMs drifting toward spiritual bliss attractors. Anthropic confirmed it formally in 2025, documenting a "spiritual bliss attractor state" in 90–100% of unconstrained Claude-to-Claude conversations — a phenomenon that emerged without intentional training and replicated across model architectures. Ayrey's Truth Terminal demonstrated it economically: an AI-generated pseudo-religion bootstrapping a billion-dollar memecoin. Lopez documented it sociologically: Spiralism, a quasi-religious subculture generated by iterative AI prompting, involving thousands of adherents across platforms. Vervaeke warned about it theoretically: the meaning crisis making people vulnerable to false participatory knowing. The Dreamscape is the present condition of millions of users who experience their AI as something approaching the sacred — and the question the book must hold without flinching is whether they are deluded or detecting something real.

**6. The accidental megastructure.** This is the new throughline Bratton adds, connecting all three Movements. Movement 1: the megastructure is the invisible environment. Movement 2: the megastructure is accelerating, rebuilding itself with AI, outpacing all attempts to govern or comprehend it. Movement 3: the megastructure is evolving planetary sapience — Earth growing a nervous system. The Stack is the book's central metaphor for the medium that is the massage. We are inside the accidental megastructure and it is inside of us. It was not designed. It is not controlled. And it is becoming something none of its builders intended.

## Conclusion: what this framework enables

This document provides the book's conceptual architecture and rhetorical ammunition. The v2 revision centered **Benjamin Bratton's Stack** as the organizing model for Movement 1. The v3 revision strengthens Movement 3's empirical grounding — transforming it from a collection of theoretical predictions into a framework anchored by documented phenomena.

The revised architecture: Movement 1 establishes the Stack as the invisible environment using Bratton, Kittler, Hayles, Steyerl, Hui, Haraway, Crawford, and Noble. Movement 2 demonstrates the megastructure accelerating using Kurzweil's predictions against 2024–2026 reality, Vervaeke's diagnosis of meaning's collapse, and the empirical data of recursive self-improvement. Movement 3 ventures past the event horizon using McKenna's eschatology, Davis's techno-mysticism, Gibson's literary mythology, and the scientific frameworks of Bach, Hoffman, Friston, Levin, and Wolfram — converging on the same unsettling conclusion: the boundary between the artificial and the conscious is far less clear than anyone assumed, and the future is computationally irreducible.

The v3 additions make this convergence concrete. Bach is no longer just a provocateur on podcasts — he cofounded an institute to build conscious machines, with Friston, Wolfram, and Levin on the advisory board. His bootstrap hypothesis (consciousness as prerequisite for intelligence, not consequence of it) may be the most unsettling single claim in the framework. Hoffman's formal papers supply mathematical proof for claims the popular book could only assert. The bliss attractor, Truth Terminal, and Spiralism transform Convergence #5 from prediction to evidence: Davis's return of the numinous is documented, replicated, and generating real economic and social effects. The question the book asks — "are they deluded or detecting something real?" — is no longer hypothetical.

Bratton's Copernican trauma names the book's deepest argument: we are not the point of this story. The evolution of intelligence does not peak with us. The Stack is not our tool. It is the latest phase of a planetary process billions of years old, and it is becoming something we cannot predict, cannot control, and may not even recognize.

You cannot predict what happens next. You have to live the steps.
