# Theorist Reference: Salient Ideas for *Medium is Massage — Revisited*

## How to Use This Document

Each theorist listed under Section 2.1 of the project plan is profiled below. For each, you'll find a one-sentence summary, the ideas most relevant to this project, notes on which of the 12 thematic domains they connect to most strongly, and a curated list of works from which direct quotes can be drawn. Works are prioritized for quotability — punchy, aphoristic, or provocative passages that match the tone of the original McLuhan/Fiore book.

---

## Friedrich Kittler (1943–2011)

**Summary:** German media theorist who radicalized McLuhan's position by arguing that media technologies don't just influence thought — they *determine* its very conditions of possibility.

**Salient Ideas:**

**Discourse Networks** — Kittler's central framework holds that every era has a "discourse network": the technological infrastructure that determines what can be said, stored, and processed. In 1800, it was the alphabet and the mother's voice teaching reading. In 1900, it was the gramophone, film, and typewriter. The contemporary discourse network is defined by computation and large language models. This is perhaps the single most important theoretical extension of McLuhan for this project: AI doesn't just carry messages — it *constitutes the conditions under which meaning is made*.

**There Is No Software** — Kittler's provocation that all software ultimately reduces to voltage differences in silicon. This collapses the distinction between "content" and "medium" even more aggressively than McLuhan did. Applied to the current work: the text a language model generates, the images a diffusion model produces — these are not "content" riding on a neutral platform. They are expressions of matrix multiplications, training data distributions, and hardware architectures. The medium is the massage at the level of the transistor.

**The Disappearance of the Human** — Kittler argued that as media technologies grow more complex, humans become increasingly peripheral to information processing. Machines write to machines. This maps directly onto the project's concern with ambient intelligence and post-literacy: we are entering (or have entered) an era where the most consequential "communication" happens between algorithms, not between people.

**Relevant Domains:** Post-Literacy & Language, Ambient Intelligence, Attention & Cognition, Agency & Autonomy

**Key Works:**

- ***Discourse Networks 1800/1900*** (1990, English trans.) — The foundational text. Dense but extraordinarily quotable in its opening and closing passages. The introduction lays out the core argument that media determine what counts as knowledge. Look especially at the sections on the typewriter and the gramophone for passages that translate directly to AI-era concerns.
- ***Gramophone, Film, Typewriter*** (1999, English trans.) — More accessible than *Discourse Networks*. The opening pages contain some of Kittler's most cited lines about media and the real. The chapter on the gramophone — on how recording technology separated sound from its source for the first time — provides powerful analogies for how AI separates intelligence from its biological substrate.
- **"There Is No Software"** (1995, essay, published in *CTheory*) — Short, polemical, and directly quotable. The core argument fits on a few pages: all software is ultimately hardware, all code is ultimately voltage. Available online. Essential for the Post-Literacy & Language domain.
- **"Protected Mode"** (1997, essay) — On the military origins of computing and how operating systems evolved from weapons systems. Useful for connecting AI's infrastructure to its military-industrial genealogy.
- ***Optical Media: Berlin Lectures 1999*** (2010, English trans.) — Kittler's most lecture-style, accessible work. The opening and closing sections contain sharp formulations about media, perception, and power that would work well as pull quotes.

## Donna Haraway (b. 1944)

**Summary:** Feminist theorist and philosopher of science whose "Cyborg Manifesto" dissolved the boundaries between human, animal, and machine, reframing identity as always already hybrid.

**Salient Ideas:**

**The Cyborg as Ontological Condition** — Haraway's cyborg isn't a future possibility; it's a description of what we already are. We are constituted by our prosthetics, our data shadows, our algorithmic profiles. In the context of AI, this idea becomes even more potent: the self is not a fixed entity that *uses* AI tools — it is continuously remade *through* its interactions with them. Every prompt shapes the prompter.

**Situated Knowledges** — Haraway's insistence that all knowledge is produced from a particular embodied position, and that claims to universal objectivity are always political. This directly challenges the rhetoric of AI as "objective" or "neutral." Every model encodes the situatedness of its training data, its designers' assumptions, its corporate context. This is essential framing for the Algorithmic Identity and Surveillance & Control domains.

**Staying with the Trouble** — Her later work argues against both techno-utopian and techno-apocalyptic narratives in favor of remaining present with the messy, entangled reality of human-nonhuman relations. This provides a tonal corrective for the project: the book should neither celebrate nor condemn AI, but sit inside the discomfort of a world being remade.

**Companion Species** — The idea that we co-evolve with our technologies (and other species) in relations of mutual constitution. AI is not a tool we wield but a companion species with which we are learning to cohabit. This reframes the entire human-AI relationship away from master/servant toward something stranger and more McLuhanesque.

**Relevant Domains:** Algorithmic Identity, Embodiment & Disembodiment, Authorship & Creativity, Agency & Autonomy

**Key Works:**

- **"A Cyborg Manifesto: Science, Technology, and Socialist-Feminism in the Late Twentieth Century"** (1985, essay, in *Simians, Cyborgs, and Women*) — The essential text. Extraordinarily quotable throughout. The opening declaration ("We are all chimeras, theorized and fabricated hybrids of machine and organism") and the closing passages on irony and blasphemy are tailor-made for a project like this. Originally published in *Socialist Review*, widely available.
- ***Staying with the Trouble: Making Kin in the Chthulucene*** (2016) — The title concept alone is useful. Chapter 1 and the conclusion contain her most direct statements about resisting both techno-optimism and techno-doom. Strong quotable passages on entanglement and response-ability.
- **"Situated Knowledges: The Science Question in Feminism and the Privilege of Partial Perspective"** (1988, essay, *Feminist Studies*) — The key text for challenging AI objectivity claims. The phrase "the god trick" — the illusion of seeing everything from nowhere — maps perfectly onto the rhetoric of AI neutrality.
- ***The Companion Species Manifesto: Dogs, People, and Significant Otherness*** (2003) — Short, punchy, and more accessible than her academic writing. The framework of "significant otherness" can be directly applied to human-AI relations.
- ***Simians, Cyborgs, and Women: The Reinvention of Nature*** (1991) — The full collection containing the Cyborg Manifesto plus surrounding essays that develop the argument. The introduction is particularly useful for contextualizing the cyborg figure.
- **"The Promises of Monsters: A Regenerative Politics for Inappropriate/d Others"** (1992, essay) — On how nature and culture, human and nonhuman, are always already entangled. Provides language for talking about AI as neither natural nor artificial but something that disrupts the distinction.

## Yuk Hui (b. 1985)

**Summary:** Hong Kong-born philosopher who argues that technology is never universal but always embedded in specific cosmological and cultural traditions, and that genuine technological pluralism — "cosmotechnics" — is essential to avoiding a homogenized digital monoculture.

**Salient Ideas:**

**Cosmotechnics** — Hui's central concept: every culture develops its own relationship between the cosmic order and technical practice. Western modernity universalized one particular cosmotechnics (Greek techne → industrial technology → computation) and imposed it globally. AI represents the apex of this universalizing tendency. The project should grapple with the question: whose intelligence is "artificial intelligence" modeled on? Whose cognition does it extend? This is a crucial corrective to McLuhan, who despite his brilliance largely assumed a Western media trajectory.

**Technodiversity** — Just as biodiversity is essential to ecological health, technodiversity is essential to cultural health. The consolidation of global AI development in a handful of corporations and research labs represents a catastrophic loss of technodiversity. The project's "Global Village, Revisited" domain should engage with this: McLuhan's global village has arrived, but it speaks English and runs on NVIDIA chips.

**Recursivity and Contingency** — Hui's work on how digital systems create recursive loops that increasingly determine their own conditions. AI systems trained on AI-generated data, recommendation algorithms that shape the behavior they then measure — these recursive dynamics are a defining feature of the current moment and map powerfully onto McLuhan's insight that environments are invisible.

**The Question Concerning Technology in China** — Hui's rereading of Heidegger through Chinese philosophy challenges the assumption that there is only one way to think about the relationship between humans and technology. For a project that aims to extend McLuhan, this is a vital reminder that "the medium" is not experienced identically across cultures.

**Relevant Domains:** The Global Village Revisited, Agency & Autonomy, Algorithmic Identity, Attention & Cognition

**Key Works:**

- ***The Question Concerning Technology in China: An Essay in Cosmotechnics*** (2016) — The foundational text for cosmotechnics. The introduction and first chapter lay out the core argument with admirable clarity. Quotable passages on the universalization of Western technology and the need for plural technological futures.
- ***Recursivity and Contingency*** (2019) — More technical and philosophical, but the introduction and conclusion contain strong formulations about recursive systems and the cybernetic feedback loops that define digital culture. The passages on how systems internalize their own outputs are directly applicable to AI training dynamics.
- **"On the Existence of Digital Objects"** (2016) — Hui's examination of what digital objects *are* — not metaphors, not representations, but entities with their own mode of existence. Useful for thinking about AI-generated images, texts, and agents as objects in their own right.
- ***Art and Cosmotechnics*** (2021) — Explores how art practices relate to different cosmotechnical traditions. Relevant for the Authorship & Creativity domain and for thinking about AI art as an expression of a specific (Western, computational) cosmotechnics.
- **"Machine and Ecology"** (2020, essay, *Angelaki*) — On the relationship between machines and ecological thinking. Contains compact, quotable arguments about why technological monoculture is as dangerous as biological monoculture.
- **"For a Planetary Thinking"** (2020, essay, *e-flux journal* #114) — Short and direct. Argues for a thinking that is both technological and ecological, global and local. Good source for pithy formulations about the need for technodiversity.
- **Interviews in *e-flux conversations* and *Philosophy & Technology*** — Hui is an active interviewee and often distills his book-length arguments into more accessible and quotable forms in conversation. Search e-flux and Strelka for transcripts.

## N. Katherine Hayles (b. 1943)

**Summary:** Literary scholar and media theorist who has traced how information lost its body in the 20th century and how, in the 21st, human cognition is increasingly distributed across "cognitive assemblages" of human and nonhuman agents.

**Salient Ideas:**

**How We Became Posthuman** — Hayles' foundational argument that the information age was built on a conceptual sleight of hand: treating information as disembodied, separable from its material substrate. This mirrors and extends McLuhan's insistence on the materiality of media. In the AI context: language models appear to "think," but their "thought" is inseparable from the specific material conditions of their training — data, compute, energy, the rare earth minerals in the GPUs.

**Cognitive Assemblages** — Hayles' more recent framework argues that cognition is not confined to human brains but distributed across networks of human and nonhuman agents. A person using ChatGPT is not a human plus a tool — they are a cognitive assemblage whose capabilities, biases, and blind spots are properties of the *system*, not of either component alone. This is perhaps the most precise contemporary articulation of McLuhan's "extensions of man."

**The Unthought** — Hayles' concept for the vast domain of nonconscious cognition performed by technical systems. Most of what AI does is "unthought" — it operates beneath or beyond human awareness. Traffic routing, content recommendation, credit scoring, predictive policing — these are cognitive processes that shape human life but are not available to human reflection. McLuhan's "invisible environment" made computationally literal.

**Hyper and Deep Attention** — Hayles' distinction between the sustained, single-focus attention fostered by print culture and the scanning, multi-stimulus attention fostered by digital media. AI collapses this further: attention is now algorithmically mediated, parceled, and sold. The user doesn't choose between hyper and deep attention — the platform chooses for them.

**Relevant Domains:** Attention & Cognition, Embodiment & Disembodiment, Post-Literacy & Language, Ambient Intelligence, Agency & Autonomy

**Key Works:**

- ***How We Became Posthuman: Virtual Bodies in Cybernetics, Literature, and Informatics*** (1999) — The prologue is one of the most quoted passages in media theory ("My nightmare is a culture inhabited by posthumans who regard their bodies as fashion accessories"). Chapter 1's account of the Macy Conferences and the erasure of embodiment provides essential historical context.
- ***Unthought: The Power of the Cognitive Nonconscious*** (2017) — The most directly relevant to this project. Chapter 1 defines cognitive assemblages with precision. The discussions of nonconscious cognition in technical systems map directly onto AI. Contains some of her most accessible and quotable writing.
- **"Hyper and Deep Attention: The Generational Divide in Cognitive Modes"** (2007, essay, *Profession*) — Short and punchy. The core distinction between attention modes is laid out clearly and provides a framework that extends naturally to AI-mediated attention.
- ***Writing Machines*** (2002) — An experimental, design-forward book (designed by Anne Burdick) that mirrors the kind of form-content integration the project is attempting. The book itself is a model for how to make the medium enact the argument. Also contains Hayles' concept of "media-specific analysis."
- ***My Mother Was a Computer: Digital Subjects and Literary Texts*** (2005) — The title essay and opening chapters explore the analogy between computational and biological processes. Contains quotable passages on code, language, and the blurring of writing and programming.
- **"Computing the Human"** (2022, essay, *Theory, Culture & Society*) — Recent work on how AI and machine learning are redefining what it means to be human. Directly addresses large language models and their implications for cognition and creativity.
- ***Postprint: Books and Becoming Computational*** (2021) — On the transformation of books and reading in the computational age. Directly relevant to the Post-Literacy & Language domain.

## Kate Crawford (b. 1976)

**Summary:** Australian researcher and writer whose *Atlas of AI* regrounds the discourse around artificial intelligence in its material, political, and ecological realities — the mines, the labor, the water, the bodies.

**Salient Ideas:**

**AI as Extractive Industry** — Crawford's central reframing: AI is not an abstraction or a purely intellectual achievement. It is a physical industry that extracts minerals from the earth, labor from human bodies, and data from human lives. This materialist corrective is essential for a project extending McLuhan, who always insisted that media have physical, bodily consequences ("the medium is the massage" — media work us over *physically*).

**The Politics of Classification** — Crawford's analysis of how AI classification systems (facial recognition, emotion detection, hiring algorithms) don't merely describe the world but actively construct categories that then become enforceable realities. Being classified as "high risk" by a predictive policing algorithm doesn't describe your criminality — it produces your surveillance. This is McLuhan's "we shape our tools and then our tools shape us" made algorithmically concrete.

**Training Sets as Ideology** — The datasets used to train AI systems are not neutral collections of facts but curated artifacts that encode particular worldviews, biases, and power structures. ImageNet, for example, reproduces and calcifies specific taxonomies of race, gender, and social hierarchy. The invisible environment is now the training set.

**Labor Invisibility** — The hidden human labor behind AI (Mechanical Turk workers, content moderators, data labelers) reveals that "artificial" intelligence depends on very real, often exploited, human intelligence. This connects to the Labor & Value domain and provides a powerful counterpoint to narratives of AI autonomy.

**Relevant Domains:** Surveillance & Control, Labor & Value, Algorithmic Identity, Embodiment & Disembodiment

**Key Works:**

- ***Atlas of AI: Power, Politics, and the Planetary Costs of Artificial Intelligence*** (2021) — The essential Crawford text. Every chapter is rich with quotable material. The opening chapter on the lithium mines of Nevada is visceral and concrete — perfect for a project that wants to ground AI in its physical reality. Chapter 3 on classification and Chapter 5 on affect recognition are particularly strong.
- **"Anatomy of an AI System"** (2018, essay/visual project with Vladan Joler) — A single-page visual map tracing all the human labor, material resources, and data required to make an Amazon Echo respond to a voice command. Originally published at anatomyof.ai. The map itself is a powerful design reference for the project, and the accompanying essay is concise and quotable.
- **"The Trouble with Bias"** (2017, NeurIPS keynote) — Crawford's most-watched public address. Accessible, direct, and contains several sharp one-liners about how bias operates in AI systems. Transcript and video available.
- **"Neither Artificial nor Intelligent"** (2021, essay, *Nature*) — A short, sharp argument that AI is better understood as an extractive industry than a form of intelligence. Extremely quotable — nearly every paragraph contains a usable line.
- **Crawford's interviews on the *Ezra Klein Show*, *Tech Won't Save Us*, and *Logic Magazine*** — She is an exceptionally clear public communicator and often produces her most punchy formulations in conversation. The Ezra Klein interview around the *Atlas of AI* launch is particularly strong.
- ***The AI Now Institute annual reports*** (2017–2019, co-authored) — While institutional in tone, these contain sharp summary statements about the state of AI governance and its failures that could serve as pull quotes.

## Hito Steyerl (b. 1966)

**Summary:** German artist and writer whose work explores how images and information circulate, degrade, and take on autonomous power in digital networks — and what this means for truth, politics, and representation.

**Salient Ideas:**

**The Poor Image** — Steyerl's concept of images that have been compressed, screenshotted, re-uploaded, and degraded through circulation. They sacrifice resolution for speed and accessibility. In the AI age, this idea extends further: AI-generated images have no "original" to degrade from. They are born poor — born as statistical averages of training distributions. The concept of image fidelity collapses entirely.

**Post-Representation** — Steyerl argues we have moved beyond an era where images represent reality into one where images *are* operational — they act in the world (facial recognition, satellite targeting, autonomous navigation). AI makes this explicit: images generated by AI are not representations of anything. They are outputs of a function. McLuhan's insight that the medium supersedes the message finds its most radical expression here.

**Duty-Free Art** — Steyerl's exploration of how art circulates in freeport storage, existing in a state of permanent transit and tax exemption — neither fully present nor absent. AI-generated creative works exist in an analogous zone: not quite authored, not quite found, not quite original, not quite derivative. This maps directly onto the Authorship & Creativity domain.

**In Free Fall** — Steyerl's metaphor for the loss of stable ground truth in digital culture. Without a horizon line, there is no way to determine orientation. AI deepfakes, synthetic media, and LLM hallucinations represent the intensification of this free fall: the ground truth doesn't just shift — it becomes computationally indeterminate.

**Relevant Domains:** Synthetic Media & Post-Truth, Authorship & Creativity, Public/Private Collapse, Surveillance & Control

**Key Works:**

- **"In Defense of the Poor Image"** (2009, essay, *e-flux journal* #10) — One of the most influential essays in contemporary art theory. Short, vivid, and endlessly quotable. The opening paragraphs alone provide material for the Synthetic Media domain. Freely available on e-flux.
- **"In Free Fall: A Thought Experiment on Vertical Perspective"** (2011, essay, *e-flux journal* #24) — The essay on loss of ground truth and the collapse of stable perspective. Contains the metaphor of perpetual free fall that maps beautifully onto the disorientation of the post-truth, AI-generated media landscape.
- ***Duty Free Art: Art in the Age of Planetary Civil War*** (2017) — The most sustained and accessible collection of her arguments. The title essay and "A Sea of Data: Pattern Recognition and Corporate Animism" are the strongest chapters for this project. "A Sea of Data" in particular contains sharp, quotable passages on how pattern recognition replaces representation.
- ***The Wretched of the Screen*** (2012) — Earlier essay collection. "In Defense of the Poor Image" appears here alongside other key texts. "Is the Internet Dead?" and "The Spam of the Earth: Withdrawal from Representation" are directly relevant — the latter on images that circulate without human audiences.
- **"Mean Images"** (2023, essay, *New Left Review* #140/141) — Her most recent and direct engagement with AI-generated imagery. Argues that AI images represent the "mean" (average) of their training data. Extremely relevant and quotable for the Synthetic Media & Post-Truth and Authorship & Creativity domains.
- **"Proxy Politics: Signal and Noise"** (2014, essay, *e-flux journal* #60) — On how digital signals stand in for (proxy for) political realities. Useful for connecting AI mediation to political representation.
- **Steyerl's artist lectures and talks** — She is a compelling and witty public speaker. Her talks at transmediale, DIS Magazine events, and various biennales are often available on YouTube and contain formulations she doesn't use in print. The ICA London talk on AI and art (2023) is particularly strong.

## Ted Chiang (b. 1967)

**Summary:** Science fiction writer whose precisely imagined thought experiments explore the philosophical implications of technology — from the nature of language to the meaning of free will — with a rigor that often exceeds academic discourse.

**Salient Ideas:**

**The Lifecycle of Software Objects** — Chiang's novella about raising digital beings (digients) explores what it means to have relationships with artificial minds that develop over time. The core insight: intelligence without experience is not intelligence. If AI is to be meaningful, it must be *situated*, not merely capable. This challenges the benchmark-driven framing of AI progress.

**Exhalation** — The title story's central metaphor — a universe powered by air pressure differentials that are gradually equalizing — serves as an allegory for entropy, thermodynamic limits, and the physical costs of computation. Crawford's materialist critique rendered as poetry.

**The Truth of Fact, the Truth of Feeling** — Chiang's story about perfect memory technology (lifelogging) explores how the ability to perfectly recall every conversation changes the nature of memory, forgiveness, and self-narrative. In the AI age: when every interaction is recorded, indexed, and retrievable, the human capacity for selective memory — essential to identity and relationships — is overridden by the machine's total recall.

**Language as World-Making** — Across Chiang's work (especially "Story of Your Life"), language is not a tool for describing reality but a system that *constitutes* the reality available to its speakers. AI language models don't just process language — they instantiate a particular relationship between language and world, one in which statistical co-occurrence replaces reference and meaning.

**Relevant Domains:** Post-Literacy & Language, Agency & Autonomy, Algorithmic Identity, Authorship & Creativity

**Key Works:**

- ***Exhalation: Stories*** (2019) — The most relevant collection. "The Lifecycle of Software Objects" (on raising AI), "Exhalation" (on entropy and the physical costs of thought), "The Truth of Fact, the Truth of Feeling" (on perfect memory and its consequences), and "Omphalos" (on the nature of evidence and belief) are all directly quotable. Chiang writes with a compression and precision that matches the tonal register of the project.
- ***Stories of Your Life and Others*** (2002) — The earlier collection. "Story of Your Life" (on language determining perception of time), "Understand" (on cognitive enhancement), and "Tower of Babylon" (on the limits of human knowledge) all provide material. "Story of Your Life" in particular provides the best literary exploration of the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis and its implications for AI language.
- **"ChatGPT Is a Blurry JPEG of the Web"** (2023, essay, *The New Yorker*) — Perhaps the single most quotable short text about large language models written to date. The compression/lossy analogy is elegant and immediately graspable. Essential reading and quoting for the Post-Literacy & Language domain.
- **"Will A.I. Become the New McKinsey?"** (2023, essay, *The New Yorker*) — On AI as a tool for capital to concentrate power. Short, sharp, and directly relevant to the Labor & Value domain.
- **"Why A.I. Isn't Going to Make Art"** (2024, essay, *The New Yorker*) — Chiang's argument that art requires intention and choice, and that current AI systems lack both. Relevant to the Authorship & Creativity domain. Contains strong quotable lines about the difference between communication and generation.
- **Interviews with Chiang in *The Verge*, *Lex Fridman Podcast*, *Kara Swisher's "On"*, and *The Ezra Klein Show*** — Chiang is a precise and careful interviewee. His conversation with Ezra Klein (2024) is particularly rich on the question of what AI does to language and meaning. The Lex Fridman conversation covers his philosophy of technology more broadly.

## James Bridle (b. 1980)

**Summary:** British artist, writer, and technologist whose work investigates how digital infrastructure and computational systems shape knowledge, politics, and ecology — often by making the invisible infrastructure visible.

**Salient Ideas:**

**The New Dark Age** — Bridle's central argument: despite having access to more information than ever before, we understand less than ever. Computation has produced not enlightenment but opacity. AI intensifies this: models are too complex for their creators to fully understand, yet they make consequential decisions about human lives. McLuhan's invisible environment rendered as epistemological crisis.

**Computational Thinking as Ideology** — Bridle argues that the spread of "computational thinking" — breaking problems into discrete, solvable components — represents not a neutral cognitive skill but a particular ideology that privileges quantification, optimization, and efficiency over other forms of understanding. When we ask AI to "solve" social problems, we are imposing this ideology.

**Cloud Infrastructure as Political Geography** — Bridle's work on data centers, undersea cables, and server farms makes visible the physical infrastructure of "the cloud." This materialist grounding parallels Crawford's work and connects to McLuhan's insistence on the physical effects of media.

**Ways of Being** — Bridle's more recent exploration of non-human intelligence (animal, ecological, computational) argues for a pluralistic understanding of intelligence that decenters human cognition. This connects to Hayles' cognitive assemblages and provides a frame for thinking about AI intelligence as genuinely different — not a lesser version of human thought but something else entirely.

**Relevant Domains:** Ambient Intelligence, Attention & Cognition, The Global Village Revisited, Agency & Autonomy

**Key Works:**

- ***New Dark Age: Technology and the End of the Future*** (2018) — The essential Bridle text for this project. The introduction and first chapter ("Chasm") contain his sharpest formulations about computational opacity. Chapter 4 ("Computation") on AI and understanding is directly relevant. The writing is vivid and quotable — Bridle comes closest of these theorists to McLuhan's own aphoristic register.
- ***Ways of Being: Animals, Plants, Machines: The Search for a Planetary Intelligence*** (2022) — Bridle's most recent and ambitious work. The chapters on octopus intelligence, mycorrhizal networks, and what these mean for our understanding of AI are unlike anything else in the literature. Strong quotable passages on intelligence as plural and distributed.
- **"Something is Wrong on the Internet"** (2017, essay, *Medium*) — Bridle's viral essay about algorithmically generated, disturbing children's content on YouTube. A concrete, visceral example of AI systems producing outcomes no human intended. Short and powerful.
- **"Drone Shadows" and "Seamless Transitions"** (art projects/essays) — Bridle's artistic practice of making invisible infrastructure visible (drawing drone outlines in public spaces, mapping rendition flight paths) provides both quotable material and design inspiration for the project. Documentation and artist statements available on jamesbridle.com.
- **"The New Aesthetic"** (2011–2013, blog series and talks) — Bridle's influential project documenting moments where the digital and physical world collide (pixelated camouflage, glitched satellite imagery, drones). The original blog posts and his SXSW talk contain sharp observations about computational vision that anticipate current AI image generation concerns.
- **Interviews on *Talking Politics*, *The Guardian Long Read podcast*, and *BBC Radio 4*** — Bridle is a lucid public communicator. His Guardian Long Read conversation around the *New Dark Age* launch distills the book's arguments into particularly quotable form.

## Jaron Lanier (b. 1960)

**Summary:** Virtual reality pioneer, musician, and technology critic who has argued for decades that the design choices embedded in digital platforms diminish human dignity, creativity, and economic agency — and that it doesn't have to be this way.

**Salient Ideas:**

**Lock-In** — Lanier's concept for how early, often arbitrary design decisions become permanent features of technological systems. MIDI locked in a particular representation of music. Social media locked in the feed, the like button, the attention economy. AI is now locking in particular representations of intelligence, creativity, and communication. Once locked in, these choices become invisible — McLuhan's invisible environment as engineering artifact.

**Digital Maoism / Hive Mind** — Lanier's critique of the ideology that collective, anonymous intelligence (Wikipedia, crowd-sourcing, AI training data) is inherently superior to individual human expression. He argues this flattens human creativity into statistical averages. Language models are the ultimate expression of this tendency: they produce the most probable next token, which is by definition the most average response.

**Data Dignity** — Lanier's proposal that people should be compensated for the data that trains AI systems. This is both a policy idea and a philosophical reframing: it insists that AI "intelligence" is not autonomous but is constituted by the uncompensated labor and creativity of millions. The medium runs on stolen massage.

**You Are Not a Gadget** — Lanier's insistence that humans adapt themselves to fit the limitations of software, rather than the reverse. We simplify our identities into profile fields, reduce our social lives to feeds, compress our creativity into formats that platforms can process. AI intensifies this: we learn to prompt in ways that produce results, and in doing so, we reshape our own thinking to match the model's expectations.

**Relevant Domains:** Algorithmic Identity, Labor & Value, Authorship & Creativity, Agency & Autonomy

**Key Works:**

- ***You Are Not a Gadget: A Manifesto*** (2010) — The most quotable Lanier book and the most tonally aligned with the McLuhan project. The opening chapter on lock-in and the "cybernetic totalist" ideology is sharp and punchy. The MIDI metaphor is a perfect, graspable example of how design choices constrain expression. The book reads almost like a manifesto — short sections, declarative statements.
- ***Who Owns the Future?*** (2013) — Lanier's argument about the economic consequences of digital networks concentrating wealth. The concept of "siren servers" — platforms that attract data and monetize it without compensating contributors — is directly relevant to AI training data debates. Chapters on "data dignity" provide the framework.
- **"Digital Maoism: The Hazards of the New Online Collectivism"** (2006, essay, *Edge*) — The original, provocative essay that launched the "hive mind" critique. Short, direct, and extremely quotable. Available on Edge.org.
- ***Ten Arguments for Deleting Your Social Media Accounts Right Now*** (2018) — Lanier at his most polemical and accessible. Each chapter is structured as a single argument, making it easy to extract pithy formulations. The chapters on behavior modification and lost free will map directly onto the project's Agency & Autonomy domain.
- ***There Is No AI*** (2024, essay published by Simon & Schuster as a short book) — Lanier's most direct engagement with AI. Argues that the framing of these systems as "artificial intelligence" is itself a design choice that obscures their actual nature as tools built from human expression. Essential and recent.
- **Lanier's interviews on *The New Yorker Radio Hour*, *Lex Fridman Podcast*, *Conan O'Brien Needs a Friend*, and *60 Minutes*** — Lanier is one of the most charismatic and quotable technology critics alive. He frequently generates formulations in conversation that don't appear in his books. His 60 Minutes appearance and the Lex Fridman interview on AI (2023) are particularly strong sources.

## Safiya Umoja Noble (b. 1971)

**Summary:** Information studies scholar who demonstrated how search engines — and by extension all AI systems — do not neutrally reflect the world but actively reproduce and amplify racial, gender, and economic hierarchies.

**Salient Ideas:**

**Algorithms of Oppression** — Noble's foundational research showing how Google search results for terms like "Black girls" returned pornographic and degrading content, revealing that search algorithms encode and amplify existing social biases. This isn't a bug — it's the structural logic of systems trained on a biased corpus and optimized for engagement. In the AI age, this extends to generative systems: image generators reproduce racial stereotypes, language models encode gender biases, hiring algorithms discriminate.

**Technological Redlining** — Noble's extension of the concept of redlining into digital space: algorithmic systems create invisible boundaries that restrict access, opportunity, and visibility along racial and economic lines. AI-driven credit scoring, predictive policing, and targeted advertising are contemporary forms of redlining that operate at scale and speed no human bureaucracy could match.

**The Myth of Neutral Technology** — Noble's persistent argument that the framing of technology as "neutral" or "objective" is itself an ideological position that serves the interests of those who build and control these systems. This connects to McLuhan's figure/ground analysis: the claim of neutrality is the ground that makes the figure of algorithmic bias invisible.

**Relevant Domains:** Algorithmic Identity, Surveillance & Control, Public/Private Collapse, The Global Village Revisited

**Key Works:**

- ***Algorithms of Oppression: How Search Engines Reinforce Racism*** (2018) — The foundational text. The introduction and Chapter 1 contain the most cited and quotable passages. Noble's description of discovering the search results for "Black girls" is concrete and visceral — exactly the kind of material that translates well to a McLuhan-style confrontational register. The conclusion provides broader framing on the politics of information infrastructure.
- **"Google Search: Hyper-visibility as a Means of Rendering Black Women and Girls Invisible"** (2013, essay, *InVisible Culture*) — The academic article that preceded the book. More concise and focused — useful for extracting the core argument in compact form.
- **"The Intersectional Internet: Race, Sex, Class and Culture Online"** (2016, co-edited with Brendesha Tynes) — The introduction provides a broader framework for understanding how digital platforms reproduce intersecting inequalities.
- **"Toward a Critical Black Digital Humanities"** (2019, essay, in *Debates in the Digital Humanities*) — A shorter piece that connects Noble's work to broader questions about knowledge production and digital scholarship.
- **Noble's talks at TED, SXSW, and her UCLA lectures** — Noble is a compelling public speaker who distills her arguments into vivid, quotable forms. Her TED talk and various keynotes are available on YouTube.
- **Interviews on *The Ezra Klein Show*, *Code Switch (NPR)*, and *Tech Won't Save Us*** — The Ezra Klein conversation is particularly useful for quotable formulations about the relationship between search, AI, and structural racism.

## Shoshana Zuboff (b. 1951)

**Summary:** Harvard business school professor who named and mapped "surveillance capitalism" — the economic system in which human experience is unilaterally claimed as free raw material for translation into behavioral data, prediction products, and ultimately behavioral modification.

**Salient Ideas:**

**Surveillance Capitalism** — Zuboff's master concept: a new economic order that claims human experience as free raw material for hidden commercial practices of extraction, prediction, and sales. The product is not data itself but *predictions of future behavior*, sold to business customers. AI is the engine of this system — it is what makes behavioral prediction at scale possible.

**Behavioral Surplus** — The data collected beyond what is needed to improve a service — the excess that is harvested for prediction. Every keystroke, hesitation, scroll speed, and abandoned cart is behavioral surplus. In the AI age, this extends to voice, gaze, facial expression, and biometric data. The massage has become total surveillance.

**Instrumentarianism** — Zuboff's term for a new form of power that operates not through violence or ideology but through the instrumental modification of behavior at scale. Not totalitarianism (which sought to own the soul) but instrumentarianism (which seeks to automate behavior). This is perhaps the most chilling extension of McLuhan's insight into the imperceptibility of media environments: you cannot resist what you cannot perceive.

**The Right to the Future Tense** — Zuboff's ethical framing: surveillance capitalism threatens the human capacity to shape the future through free will. When behavior is predicted and nudged with sufficient precision, the future becomes predetermined. The question of agency — central to the project's thematic domains — is reframed as a question of temporal sovereignty.

**Relevant Domains:** Surveillance & Control, Agency & Autonomy, Public/Private Collapse, Labor & Value, Algorithmic Identity

**Key Works:**

- ***The Age of Surveillance Capitalism: The Fight for a Human Future at the New Frontier of Power*** (2019) — The magnum opus. At 700+ pages it is not concise, but Zuboff writes with a moral urgency that produces genuinely quotable passages. The introduction ("Home or Exile in the Digital Future") and Chapter 1 contain the core definitions. Part III on "instrumentarian power" is the strongest section for this project. The final chapter, "A Coup from Above," contains her most forceful language.
- **"Big Other: Surveillance Capitalism and the Prospects of an Information Civilization"** (2015, essay, *Journal of Information Technology*) — The academic article that introduced "surveillance capitalism" as a concept. More compact than the book and useful for extracting the core argument in quotable form.
- **"The Coup We Are Not Talking About"** (2021, essay, *The New York Times*) — A short, direct op-ed written after the January 6 insurrection, connecting surveillance capitalism to the erosion of democracy. Punchy and accessible.
- **"You Are the Object of a Secret Extraction Operation"** (2021, essay, *The New York Times*) — Another sharp op-ed. The title alone is nearly usable as a pull quote.
- **"Surveillance Capitalism and the Challenge of Collective Action"** (2019, essay, *New Labor Forum*) — Connects surveillance capitalism to labor and collective organization. Useful for the Labor & Value domain.
- **Zuboff's interviews on *The Ezra Klein Show*, *Channel 4 News*, *Joe Rogan Experience*, and her keynote at the IAPP Global Privacy Summit** — Zuboff is an extraordinarily passionate public speaker. Her Ezra Klein conversation and Channel 4 interview contain some of her most compressed and powerful formulations. The phrase "the right to the future tense" appears most forcefully in her public speaking.
- **"The Age of Surveillance Capitalism" Google Talk** (2019, YouTube) — Zuboff's own presentation of the book's argument. Contains her clearest, most audience-friendly articulations of the key concepts.

## Cross-Cutting Observations for the Project

**The Invisible Environment Is Now Computational.** McLuhan's foundational insight — that the most powerful effects of media are environmental and therefore invisible — finds its contemporary expression across almost every theorist listed here. Kittler's discourse networks, Hayles' unthought, Zuboff's instrumentarianism, Bridle's new dark age, and Noble's myth of neutrality all describe the same phenomenon from different angles: computation has become the environment, and its effects are structurally unavailable to casual perception.

**The Body Keeps Coming Back.** Despite (or because of) the disembodying tendencies of digital culture, these theorists repeatedly insist on materiality. Crawford's mines and labor, Haraway's embodied situatedness, Hayles' insistence that information was never actually disembodied, Lanier's protest that we are not gadgets. The project should follow McLuhan's lead in foregrounding the body — the *massage* — as the site where technological change is registered.

**Classification Is Power.** Noble, Crawford, Zuboff, and Steyerl all converge on the insight that AI's primary social function is *classification* — sorting people, images, behaviors, and possibilities into categories. These categories then become enforceable realities. McLuhan's "we shape our tools and then our tools shape us" is now operating at the speed and scale of computation.

**Pluralism vs. Monoculture.** Hui's cosmotechnics, Lanier's critique of lock-in, Bridle's call for alternative intelligences, and Haraway's companion species all argue for *diversity* — of technologies, of ways of knowing, of forms of intelligence. The consolidation of AI development into a handful of corporations and paradigms represents a threat not just to competition but to epistemological pluralism.

**The Recursive Loop.** Hui, Hayles, and Steyerl all identify a distinctive feature of the current moment: systems that feed back into themselves. AI trained on AI-generated data. Recommendation engines that shape the behavior they then measure. Prediction markets that influence the outcomes they predict. McLuhan's "rear-view mirror" — our habit of understanding new media through the lens of old ones — has been automated and accelerated into a hall of mirrors.
